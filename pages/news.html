California Laws

CPPA Issues First Major Enforcement Fines Under CPRA

The California Privacy Protection Agency has issued its first significant fines under the CPRA, targeting three major data brokers that failed to honor deletion requests within the required 45-day window. Fines ranged from $100,000 to $1.2 million per company — a clear signal that the CPPA is moving from guidance into active enforcement. This matters for teens because data brokers often hold detailed profiles built from social media activity, location history, and browsing habits.

Teen & Minors

Instagram Expands Teen Account Protections Nationwide

Following pressure from California regulators under the Age-Appropriate Design Code Act, Meta has expanded its "Teen Accounts" feature to all US users under 18. New accounts now default to private, restrict messaging from strangers, and include built-in daily screen time limits. Critics note that existing accounts weren't automatically converted — only new sign-ups get the protections by default, leaving millions of existing teen users without the changes unless they opt in manually.

Education Platform Breach Exposes 4 Million Student Records

A widely used school learning management platform disclosed a breach affecting over 4 million K–12 and college students, exposing names, email addresses, grades, and in some cases home addresses. Under California law, affected students must be notified within 72 hours — but many reported not hearing anything for over two weeks. The California AG's office has opened an investigation into the delayed notification, which could result in additional fines on top of breach-related liabilities.

California Laws

New Bill Would Force Schools to Audit EdTech Privacy Practices

A newly introduced California bill, AB 1291, would require all K–12 school districts to conduct annual privacy audits of every third-party educational technology tool they use — including apps, websites, and AI tutoring platforms. Schools would have to publish results publicly and notify parents when a tool collects sensitive student data. Advocates say this is especially critical as AI-powered learning tools become more widespread in classrooms without adequate oversight.

Teen & Minors

EFF Report: AI Companion Apps Retaining Teens' Most Personal Disclosures

Privacy researchers at the Electronic Frontier Foundation found that several AI companion and tutoring apps popular with teenagers collect and retain conversation logs indefinitely — including personal disclosures about mental health, relationships, and family situations. Under California's CPRA, this qualifies as "sensitive personal information" requiring explicit consent. Several of the apps had no clear consent mechanism for users under 18, putting them in potential violation of both CPRA and the Age-Appropriate Design Code.

Ongoing Debates

The Biggest Privacy Questions Right Now

These debates shape the laws of tomorrow — and they affect you directly.

01

Will the US Finally Get a Federal Privacy Law?

Congress is once again debating a national privacy law that would set a baseline across all 50 states. The central fight is whether a federal law would override stronger state protections like California's CCPA — a move California lawmakers strongly oppose. Privacy advocates argue that without a strong federal floor, the patchwork of state laws leaves most Americans with far fewer protections than Californians currently enjoy.

02

AI and Your Personal Data: Who Owns What You Tell a Chatbot?

As millions of teens use AI tools for homework, creative writing, and emotional support, a major question has emerged: can companies use your conversations to train future AI models? Most terms of service allow this by default. California regulators are now examining whether CPRA's opt-out rights apply to AI training data — a ruling that could reshape how every major AI company operates.

03

Age Verification Online: Privacy Protection or Privacy Risk?

Several states have passed laws requiring social media platforms to verify users' ages before sign-up — but privacy experts warn that age verification itself creates major risks, since it typically requires uploading a government ID. California is weighing an approach that puts the burden on platforms rather than users, potentially through device-level age signals rather than document uploads. No clear solution exists yet.

04

School Surveillance Tech: Safety Tool or Privacy Violation?

Many California school districts now use AI-powered monitoring software that scans students' devices for signs of self-harm, bullying, or threats — including flagging private messages and search history. Supporters say it saves lives; critics argue it normalizes surveillance for an entire generation. A pending California bill would require schools to notify students and parents any time a monitoring alert is reviewed by a human administrator.
